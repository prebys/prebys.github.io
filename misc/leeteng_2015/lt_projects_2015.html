5. Simulations Studies of the Low Energy Injector at ASTA


Mentor: Charles Thangaraj/Alexander Valishev
Student: Tianyi Ge, UCLA

The student will write extensive python code to simulate the low energy injector at ASTA. The goal of this code is to be able to track, match, study space-charge effects, study bunch compression and error/jitter studies on the low energy injector lattice with an emphasis on practical 'machine studies'. The code should be usable by the machine operator to conduct quick analytical simulations to check with experiment. Detailed nature of the limits of experiments has to be integrated. Laser shaping and other additions will be included as an option. At the end of this, the student will have a very good understanding of modern linear accelerators and the physics of low energy electron beams.

Requirements:
Good analytical skills
Some programming background

6. Gas-filled Hadron Monitor for Long Baseline Neutrino Facility (LBNF)

Mentor: Katsuya Yonehara
Student: Holly Dinkel, University of Missouri-Columbia

The Long Baseline Neutrino Facility (LBNF) is the future flagship experiment at Fermilab. A hadron flux monitor plays an important role to measure quality of the secondary charged particles and to precisely direct the beam to the neutrino detectors located hundreds of kilometers away. However, the standard hadron monitor based on an ionization chamber has a limited lifetime.  A novel gas-filled RF-resonator beam profile monitor is proposed.

It will be simple structure and, therefore radiation-robust in extremely high radiation environments. Analytical physics model in the gas-filled RF resonator shows that the permittivity of gas will be changed by the incident charged particles that is enough sensitivity with the LBNF beam profile.

We will design the proof-of-principle test at MTA in Fermilab to verify the concept of new hadron monitor.  Summer student start estimating the heat deposition of intense MTA beam into the monitor by using The analytical and numerical calculations. The student will also participate the present gas-filled RF experiment at the MTA to understand the physics in the gas-filled RF resonator.


Requirements:
Basic physics knowledge
Analytical skills
Interest in detector technology

2. Stability of Notch Filter for RF Feedback

Mentor(s): Tim Berenc
Student: Trevor Vannoy, Montana State University

Radio-frequency (rf) systems employ feedback to regulate both the amplitude and phase of the accelerating voltage in a particle accelerator. An adaptive notch filter is presently being investigated for the Advanced Photon Source (APS) storage ring rf systems [1]. This project will consist of a detailed and thorough stability analysis of the adaptive notch filter system, including experimental studies. Such an analysis will also be applicable to rf systems which employ feedback of a similar form as the adaptive notch filter. This allows analysis of the multi-input multi-output (MIMO) system to be reduced to the analysis of a single-input single-output (SISO) system.

Requirements:

lectrical engineering student with coursework in feedback control theory, signal processing (i.e., fast fourier transforms and power spectral densities), and RF concepts. Background in complex analysis/number theory is a plus.
Strong motivation and independence in experiment and data analysis. Experience with MATLAB/Simulink, LabView, and FPGA programming.

3.  Development and Fabrication of half-wave Resonator

Mentor(s): Zachary Conway
Student: Nathan Prins, Towson University

High-intensity superconducting radio-frequency proton and heavy-ion accelerators are being developed for medical, industrial, national-security, and basic research applications.  Experimental and theoretical work is underway to precisely define the beam- currents, beam-energy, and operating conditions for the separate application scenarios.  Examples of much of the technology required to build the high-energy sections (0.4 – 1 GeV per nucleon) of these accelerators have been demonstrated and are routinely fabricated.  In this project you will help with the fabrication and assembly of a new half-wave resonator cryomodule for the Fermilab PIP-II project, a high intensity H- accelerator.  You will be exposed to many aspects of experimental physics and mechanical engineering in this project: handling of radio-frequency superconducting devices, precision machining and manufacturing, high-vacuum systems, low-particulate cleaning and clean-room technology, magnetic shielding, and thermal transport.

Requirements:
Familiar with hand and machine tools.  Experience with rough vacuum systems.

4.  Data Acquisition Script for Envelope Detector Module

Mentor(s): Richard Farnsworth
Student: Jialun Luo, Carleton College

This project involves the development and testing of data aquisition code for an envelope detector module. The test script will allow the user to scan data from the detector's eight channels one by one. The  script will gather, linearize and save the data from the detector in an organized file. An existing python based software suite called PyEpics will be used to control and access the channels of the detector. The software developer will become to be familiar with Python as well as with the EPICS software suite.

Requirements:
Computer science. Detectors, software, scripting.

5. Solid State RF Power Amplifiers

Mentor(s): Goeff Waldschmidt/Doug Horan
Student: Christofer Fadden, Georgia Institute of Technology

The Lee Teng student will primarily work on the design and evaluation of solid state rf amplifiers for the APS where the student will learn the fundamentals of power amplifier design and analysis.  That knowledge will then be applied to a practical design using the ADS software package to verify and optimize its performance.  The student will also participate in the physical layout of lumped circuit matching and coupling elements on a printed circuit board and in the testing of the device.

Skills:
Circuit course. Microstrip and amplifer design.

Lee Teng Projects 2011

Fermilab

  • Material Data Compilation for Modeling of Superconducting Magnets
    • Mentors: Dariusz Bocian, Giorgio Ambrosio, Technical Division
    • Intern: Andrew Davies, St. Cloud State University
    • Description: A Network Model is used to study the thermal behavior of magnet coils and to calculate the quench levels of the superconducting magnets. One of the vital components of the model is the cryogenic materials data used in simulation.  Materials data are essential input of Network Model. In order to prepare this input and to study of model sensitivity on different material properties parameterizations a material data compilation is required. The collected data will be implemented into thermal magnet model. Then, the simulated data with Network Model will be compared with available experimental results.  The results of the work will be used to calculate the thermal behavior of superconducting magnets as well as to assess the magnet components crucial to heat transfer in the magnet. 
  • Superconducting RF (A0)
    • Mentor: Elvin Harm, Helen Edwards, Accelerator Division
    • Intern: Cole Cook, University of Wisconsin, Eau Claire
    • Description: Assist in analysis of test results and participate in future test of singe cell Superconducting RF cavities. There is a modest R&D program here to both understand performance and investigate the underlying physics of these devices. 
  • Beam dynamics with deflecting mode cavity
    • Mentor: Tanaji Sen, Accelerator Physics Center
    • Intern: Keren Li, Stonybrook University
    • Description: Beam dynamics with deflecting cavity or longitudinal bunch dynamics
  • Detection of the Electron Cloud with Ultraviolet Light 
    • Mentor: Bob Zwaska, Accelerator Physics Center
    • Intern: Sarah Pfluger, Illinois State University
    • Description:This project focuses on using ultraviolet light as an alternative detection technique for the election cloud.  Light will be emitted as the electrons bombard the surface.  The advantage of this technique is that it may allow imaging of the impact process within a dipole magnet where there is no room for other equipment.  The student will use an electron gun and photospectrometer to benchmark this effect, and work on a concept for implementaiton in the Main Injector.
  • Microwave Transmission Technique for Measuring E-cloud
    • Mentore: Charles Thangaraj, Acclerator Physics Center
    • Intern: Hexuan Wang, Mt. Holyoke University
    • Description:For the summer project, the student will work on the setting up standing waves inside an elliptical pipe.  The test stand with a beam pipe will be provided.  The major work of the student is to measure transmission properties of the elliptical beam pipe for various end aperture geometry using a network analyzer.  The student will systematically vary the orientation of the aperture (horizontal or vertical), polarization of the electric field (vertical or horizontal) width and the frequency of the exciting field and measure the reflection coefficient for these settings. The student should be able to experimentally show the aperture widths, orientation and electric field polarization for which the desired goals of the reflection coefficients are achieved.  At the end of the work, the student will write a report and present the results to the electron cloud group in Fermilab. Through this project, the student will gain good knowledge on modern accelerators and its issues and gain experimental skills on RF measurements.

  • Storage ring emittance optimization with longitudinal dipole gradient  
    • Mentor: Chun-xi Wang
    • Intern: TBD

  • EPICS Device Driver for an Insertion Device
    • Mentor: Richard Farnsworth (AES Controls)
    • Intern: TBD
    • Description:EPICS is a set of open source software tools for the distributed control of scientific instruments such as particle accelerators, telescopes, and large scientific experiments. This project involves writing an EPICS Device driver for use on an insertion device, which is the source of the bright x-rays at the APS. The project will involve the student learning about EPICS and in particular the techniques involving driver templates called the ASYN device drive or the Stream I/O driver. They will be asked to research the problem before arriving as EPICS is well documented and is available open source. The device for which the student has developed the driver will be used on an insertion device. The Student will be asked to exhaustively test the driver in the Labs at the APS and under the supervision of experienced engineers. 
    • Skills:Exposure or experience with C, C++, Java, or scripting languages is good. Experience with Linux (or other UNIX) is a plus. 
  • Nanoradian Slope Measuring Profiler Development OR Nanofocusing Mirror Performance Simulation 
    • Mentor: Lahsen Assoufid (XSD Optics/Detectors) 
    • Intern: TBD
    • Description1: The project involves assisting in developing a nanoradian slope measuring profiler for x-ray mirror surface characterization. X-ray mirrors are devices used to collimate and focus the x-ray beams at the APS and this project is centered around a new state-of-the-art instrument critical to quantifying the surface quality of beamline mirrors. To put things into perspective a nanoradian angle would only yield enough field of view to watch a 15” television from the moon!
    • Skills1: Requires (a little strong I think CAP) knowledge of basic optics and data acquisition and control using LabView.
    • Description 2: This project involves assisting in developing an existing wave optics computer program to simulate the performance of nanfocusing x-ray mirrors. Nanofocusing optics are important components on APS bealines, allowing scientists to delve into the behaviors of materials at the nanoscale. The code is written in both IDL and Fortran versions.
    • Skills 2:Analytical programming experience is a plus, as is exposure to Fortran or IDL.

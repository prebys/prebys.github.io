Proton Improvement Plan III (PIP-III)

General

PIP-III is currently a generic term used to capture activity related to proposed activities to increase the high energy (60-120 GeV) proton power from the Fermilab Main Injector beyond the 1.2 MW goal of PIP-II. The goal of this effort is to set a performance baseline for long term planning, as well as to identify open questions which will require R&D resources to answer in the near term.

Reference Specifications

PIP-II personel have been considering future improvements for some time, and have reached the tentative conclusion that the most promising baseline approach to consists of
  • Replacing the existing Booster with a 20 Hz rapid cycling synchrotron (RCS). 
  • Eliminate the Recycler as a preloader, and inject directly into the Main Injector.  It was felt that the aperture restriction of the Recycler represents a significant design challenge, and the new booster can be designed in such a way as to eliminate the need or preloading.  This choice also leaves open the option of possibly injecting into the Main Injector at a higher energy than 8 GeV.
  • Setting a target of 2.4 MW at 120 GeV and at least 2 MW at 60 GeV.
Reference specications for this machine, as well as alternative scenarios can be found in this spreadsheet, produced by Valeri Lebedev, Paul Derwent, and Steve Holmes.  A summary of the beam requirements can be found in this document.

Main Areas of Research

The key areas of research should focus on the following areas.
  • Most importantly, is 2.4 MW overly conservative?  This should be determined soon enough to influence design decisions for LBNF.  This of course depends on addressing all the questions below.
  • Determining whether the new RCS will be injected directly from the PIP-II 800 MeV linac, or whether an additional linac will be added to increase the injection energy to 2 GeV.  This decision depends largely on whether a viable technique can be found to mitigate space charge tuneshift at injection into the RCS.  Promising approaches include the following, which will both be studied extensively  in the IOTA ring, at the FAST facility:
    • integrable non-linear optics
    • electron lenses
  • Determining the optimum circumference for the RCS.  Estimates so far have assumed that it will have the same circumference as the existing Booster (1/7th of the Main Injector), but a larger circumference would reduce the loading time.
  • Determining the optimum injection energy for the Main Injector.  Current estimates assume it remains at 8 GeV, but raising it could reduce cycle times and possibly mitigate space charge issues.
  • Loading, space charge, and electron cloud issues in the Main Injector
Once the energy range and key features of the RCS are determined, the more detailed design  can continue.

Alternatives

Possible alternatives would include:
  • Replacing the RCS with a pulsed linac.  This would certainly be a more expensive and technically complex option, which would only be justified if there is a physics motivation for a high power 8 GeV proton beam.
  • Retaining the Recycler.  This would be an option if it proved impractical to increase the per batch intensity of the new RCS to compensate for the Main Injector loading time; however, given both the aperture restrictions of the Recycler and the fact we are designing the RCS from scratch, this seems like a very unlikely scenario.

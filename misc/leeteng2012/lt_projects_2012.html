Fermilab Projects

1.  Microwave measurement of e-cloud using resonant cavity technique

Mentor(s): Charles Thangaraj, Bob Zwaska
Student: Phyo Aung Kyaw

The goal of this experimental work will be to extend the bench setup on the microwave measurements with proper boundary conditions, slit orientation and antenna direction. After measuring and benchmarking certain parameters, the student will add two short stubs on the pipe ends to match the boundary conditions in an real experiments  and make phase-shift measurements. One of the objective of this work will be to find out what is the minimum "ear width"  that will maximize the phase shift signal. At the end, the student will submit a report on this work and present to the e-cloud group.

2. Design of the 2nd bunch compressor in ASTA

Mentor(s): Tanaji Sen, Philip Piot
Student: Druv Kedar

In the first phase of ASTA (Advanced Superconducting Test Accelerator)  the electron bunch will be compressed at a relatively low energy (~40 MeV) resulting in a significant deterioration of the bunch transverse phase space due to collective effects such as space charge and coherent synchrotron radiation. The plan is therefore to compress the bunch in two stages. The 40-MeV compression stage will be used to partially compress the bunch with tolerable phase space degradation and a second bunch compressor will need to be installed after the bunch has been accelerated to higher energies. The intern will explore possible designs for the second-stage bunch compressor. A simple one-dimensional model will initially be used to investigate and optimize the performance of a two-stage bunch compression thereby leading to requirements on the second-stage bunch compressor. Simulations including some of the collective effects with the ELEGANT-code model of ASTA will be performed for further optimization of the second bunch compressor.

3. Multiparticle beam dynamics simulations of ASTA 

Mentor(s):  Philip Piot, Tanaji Sen
Student: Yuan Hui Wu

At  ASTA, bunches containing up to 2E10 electrons will be generated from the photoinjector. Such high-charge medium-energy (~40 MeV) bunches are prone to collective effects such as space charge. The dynamical evolution of bunches needs to properly account for these collective effects. The intern will develop an independent model of the ASTA photoinjector using the PARMELA particle tracking program. This independent model will be bench-marked against previous simulations and will be further refined by including some collective effects available in PARMELA including the bunch self-interaction with its radiated field (via wakefields and coherent synchrotron radiation). The numerical simulation model developed will then be used to perform parametric studies of the bunch parameters for various bunch charge and photoinjector configurations.

4. Investigation and Operation of Superconducting RF Systems

Mentor(s):  Elvin Harms, Mike Church
Student: Jian Cong Zeng

A second 8-cavity Superconducting RF cryomodule will be installed and brought into operation at NML. Since this is the first such device built entirely under Fermilab's direction, there will be a complete dataset of test measurements made on the cavities and couplers. As performance measurements are made on the cavities and input couplers, it will be important to compare these test results against previous single cavity ones to determine any degradation in performance and possible sources. It will be necessary to both participate in upcoming tests and access data from previous ones.

5. SRF cavity studies

Mentor(s):  Anna Grassellino,Camille Ginsberg
Student: Qinxia Wang

Superconducting RF accelerating cavities are an important component of all conceived future accelerator projects at Fermilab.  While their performance has improved dramatically in recent years, some common performance limitations
are known and must be repaired before cavities may be installed into an accelerator.  The student will participate in cavity qualification studies, and contribute to the study of specific performance limitations using thermometry and other diagnostic instrumentation, as well as being introduced to repair techniques.  The student will learn the basic accelerator physics and fundamental superconducting materials physics associated with SRF cavity acceleration.

6. The New Ion Source and RFQ at the Fermilab Linac

Mentor(s):  Bob Zwaska, C.Y. Tan, Dan Bollinger
Student: Purnima Balakrishnan

Fermilab is replacing its ion source and Cockcroft Walton accelerators with a new ione source and RFQ.  These new accelerators need to be fully characterized in terms of their beam energy, distributions, and operating conditions.  This work involves high power RF, beam instrumentation, and software reconstruction.

7. Fast optical spectrometer to study electric breakdown in RF cavities

Mentor(s):  Katsuya Yonehara, Moses Chung
Student: Adam Sibley

The electric breakdown mechanism in an RF cavity remains a mystery.  The plasma dynamics is an essential to investigate the mechanism.   In past measurement, the time evolution of plasma formation was taken by using a streak camera.  However, it is very expensive and takes a picture only in a very short time period.   We propose to make a fast optical spectrometer based on a high sensitive SiPM.  We will investigate what the light source is generated in the breakdown.  We also plan to put a light emitter in the cavity to investigate energy distribution of plasma.

8. Experiments with magnetically confined electron beams

Mentor(s):  Giulio Stancari
Student: Siqi Li

Experiments in the Fermilab Tevatron collider have shown that
low-energy, magnetically confined hollow electron beams can be used to
control the halo of the circulating beam. Electron guns developed for
this purpose are tested in the Fermilab electron-lens test stand. The
Lee Teng intern will design improvements to the experimental setup and
characterize the properties of the hollow electron beam.

3.  Cryomodule Test System

Mentor(s): Genfa Wu, Goeff Waldschmidt
Student: Stephen Bauman

ANL is developing superconducting cryomodule for short pulse X-ray generation (SPX0). The critical component of SPX0 cryomodule is a deflecting cavity made of superconducting material: niobium. The niobium cavity will be processed chemically and heat treated in UHV vacuum furnace at 600 C. Its performance will be tested under superfluid liquid helium temperature (2K) before it can be integrated into a final cryomodule.  The effort will be focused on horizontal cavity test, where one needs to simulate the thermal profiles of RF waveguides leading to SRF cavity in a cryostat. A cavity test will help verify the simulation result and improve the simulation model to assist the similar design of cryomodule components. Some existing labview software needs to be improved to support this test. To support the cavity test, a simple thermometer data acquisition using carbon thermometers is being developed. The experiment uses constant current source; an accurate voltage measurement at low temperature is required. Some signal processing is needed to improve the accuracy. 

4. Developing an improved machine model for ATLAS

Mentor(s): Richard Pardo
Student: Taylor Clarke

ATLAS (the Argonne Tandem-Linac Accelerator System) is a national user
facility for nuclear physics research.  Experimenters from all over the
world come to Argonne to conduct experiments in in nuclear astrophysics,
nuclear structure and material science with the heavy ion beams from the
ATLAS Accelerator.  Every few days the beam delivered changes and can range
from protons to uranium.  This summer project will develop an improved
accelerator beam modeling program that more seamlessly merges the
accelerator control system with an off-line simulation tool known as TRACK.
The student will not need to perform 'hard core' programming, but will learn
to access information from databases, automatically create a text file and
work with accelerator equipment to perform calibrations and them compare
actual performance to model predictions.

5. High QE Photocathode

Mentor(s): Zeke Yusof
Student: Tapash Sarkar

The project will consist of two areas. The student will first learn about
ultra-high vacuum systems and standard maintenance of such a system.
Secondly, the student will learn about the fabrication/properties of a
high-QE photocathode for accelerator photoinjector. This will include the
growth of cesium telluride photocathodes that will be used in the Argonne
Wakefield Accelerator.

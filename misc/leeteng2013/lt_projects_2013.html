Fermilab Projects

1.  Installation and Commissioning of the Radiabeam Profile Monitors at ASTA

Mentor(s): Amber Johnson, John Diamond
Student: John Nocita, Kalamazoo College

This summer we anticipate the installation 10 transverse profile monitors in the low energy beamline at the Advanced Superconducting Test Accelerator (ASTA).  An important diagnostic for the 50 MeV electron beam is optical transition radiation (OTR) foils and YAG crystal scintillators.  Each Radiabeam station is populated with an OTR foil, either a YAG or LYSO scintillator, and a target pattern.  Once the vacuum portion is installed, the student will be required to verify proper actuation and set limit switches on target holders.  Software to record images is currently in development and we will need to confirm functionality with the 4 different camera models to be installed.  Once images are recorded we will use Matlab to process the data and check that the resolution requirement of 20 microns is met.

2. Tomographic Studies of the Longitudinal Phase Space

Mentor(s): Duncan Scott
Student: Tegan Johnson, Otterbein University

During the current year long shut-down new software is being developed to reconstruct the longitudinal phase-space of the Main Injector and Recycler beam using data acquired from resistive-wall current monitors. These detectors generate 1-D profiles of the charge distribution and multiple profiles, taken over many turns around the machine, can be used with tomography algorithms to reconstruct the longitudinal phase-space. The profiles can also be analysed to extract other information, such as single- and coupled-bunch modes of the beam, beam loading effects, etc. The reconstructions can be used as an online diagnostic tool and as input for further modelling of the beam. The code is written in c++ using Fermilab and CERN libraries with a number of modules on data acquisition, pre-processing raw data, particle tracking, reconstruction algorithms, data analysis, visualization, a graphical user interface, etc.

As this is a new code, still untested with the working accelerator, there are many opportunities for developing, testing and benchmarking.  A well-motivated student will be able to find many areas to contribute based on their own preferences. Examples of possible projects include:

*    Implementing a filter algorithm during data pre-processing to account for signal dispersion from the detector.
*    Investigating the effect of the Main Injector beam loading compensation.
*    Using the reconstructions to "measure" beam and accelerator parameters, such as the beam energy, accelerating voltage, transition gamma, etc.   
*    Implementing different particle tracking algorithms.
*    Generating an animation of the evolution the longitudinal phase space
*    Linking the outputs of the tomographic reconstruction with other codes to further model the beam
*    General bug-fixing and testing during accelerator operations and code optimization,

Experience of c/c++ and/or other programming languages is essential. Knowledge of accelerator physics is beneficial but not necessary.  

3. Channeling Radiation at HBESL

Mentor(s):  Tanaji Sen, Philippe Piot
Student: Christopher Lynn, Swarthmore College

Compact X-ray sources are in great demand for a variety of uses such as medical imaging, physics research and detection of illicit materials. The high brightness electron source  laboratory (HBESL) at A0 will use an electron beam from a photoinjector and a diamond crystal to generate X-rays via channeling radiation. The summer project will involve theoretical calculation of the expected spectral distribution of the radiation and comparison with experimental data. The calculation will be done with a Mathematica code which solves the relevant one dimensional Schrodinger equation to find the bound state eigenfunctions and the energy levels. Familiarity with Mathematica, undergraduate quantum mechanics and electrodynamics is required.

5. Study of surface electric breakdown of ceramic loaded gas-filled RF test cell

Mentor(s):  Katsuya Yonehara, Maria Leonova
Student: Yiqing Ding, Purdue University

A ceramic loaded RF cavity is attractive since it can be smaller volume than the conventional pillbox cavity. An electron cascade is processed on the surface of loaded ceramic in high RF gradient. As a result, it induces
the RF breakdown at lower RF gradient than the dielectric strength of ceramics. We propose to fill a buffer gas  in the cavity to terminate the electron cascade. We will measure the RF signal and the breakdown light.
Student will join the experiment and make some analysis by using analysis code (Mathematica, Root, or custom-made written by the standard computer language).

Argonne Projects

1.  Computer program to simulate the performance of nanofocusing x-ray mirrors

Mentor(s): Lahsen Assoufid
Student: Haoyu Sun, University of California, Berkeley

Participate in developing an existing wave optics computer program to simulate the performance of nanofocusing x-ray mirrors. The code is written in both  IDL and FORTRAN versions. The work includes participating in the metrology measurements of a focusing x-ray mirror fabricated at the APS and analysis of the obtained data using the above mentioned computer code. The goal is to study and quantify the effect of fabrication error on of the performance of the mirror.

The work requires knowledge of basic optics, advanced algebra and geometry, and programing in FORTRAN, IDL or Matlab.

2.  Interferometric phase noise measurement system

Mentor(s): Tim Berenc
Student: Pakpoom Buabthong, University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign

The Short-Pulse X-ray project at Argonne National Laboratory demands state-of-the-art differential phase noise tolerances from multiple superconducting radio-frequency (SRF) cavities.  The low-level RF control system plans on using a calibration tone to suppress cable drift and 1/f noise of the receiver front end [1].  To characterize the benefit of this process a residual phase noise measurement system needs to have a sufficiently low noise floor at low frequency offsets from the RF carrier.  This project would entail building a basic form of a RF interferometric phase noise measurement system [2] based upon our existing equipment.

Electrical engineering student with coursework in signal processing (i.e., fast fourier transforms and power spectral densities) and RF concepts.  Strong motivation and independence in experiment and data analysis

References

3.  CARIBU electron beam ion source (EBIS) charge breeder

Mentor(s): Clayton Dickerson
Student: Kelsey O'Connor, Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University

The CARIBU electron beam ion source (EBIS) charge breeder will accept and trap ion bunches within a magnetically confined electron beam.  The injected ions will reach higher and higher ionization levels due to electron impact ionization events between the energetic main electron beam and the electrons bound to the ions.  The work for this project will involve commissioning and testing the EBIS and its subsystems.  Vacuum chambers will be assembled and aligned.  High voltage power supplies will be installed and connected to the appropriate electrodes.  The controls software will be written to properly monitor and sequence the various systems.  Ultimately the performance of the EBIS will be benchmarked as the subsystems are installed and brought online.  We are looking for a candidate that can assistance in as many areas as possible.Accelerator operations, familiarity with vacuum systems, controls experience, mechanical assembly experience, electronics experience

Accelerator operations, familiarity with vacuum systems, controls experience, mechanical assembly experience, electronics experience.

4. Design of RF MgB2 coated cavities

Mentor(s): Ali Nassiri, Robert Kustom
Student: Lauren Perez, Florida International University

The student would participate in studies on designing RF MgB2 coated cavities that would be compatible with 10-12K operation using cryocoolers instead of liquid cryogents.  The study would involve calculating RF heat loads generated in the cavity walls for various cavity shapes and configurations and integrating the study with thermohydraulic calculations performed by a cryogenic scientist (probably Yuko Shiroyanagi). This project is a new LDRD program which is in the early stages of evolution, and many specifics are not yet decided, however, an ILC cavity does exist for early testing and would be the starting point for thermal design and analysis, EM excitation, etc. Other ideas will undoubtedly develop with further analysis.  

The student should have a few years of college physics and mathematics with at least an entry level course on EM theory, and more advanced courses would be even better. Of course, the student should also be sufficiently computer literate to learn and apply EM programs with some reasonable amount of guidance.

5. Flow-Induced Vibration of X-ray Optics

Mentor(s): Curt Preissner, Jeff Collins
Student: Zichen Xiao, University of Southern California

The focus of this project is to investigate flow-induced vibration of X-ray optics, specifically the effects of a new type of hose. These Clear Flow Flex turbulence suppressive low-vibration flexible hoses have a special type of liner that is designed to reduce the level of vibration caused by the liquid flowing through the hose. The objectives of the project are to determine the benefit, if any, from the new hose and gain an understanding of how the flow conditions cause vibration.


The student will use a laboratory flow measurement system to measure the level of vibration on a test optic for a variety of hose types and flow conditions, including a control case. The measurements will be made with water and with liquid nitrogen if time permits. In the cases where the fluid is water, the dynamic pressure fluctuations along the channel wall will be measured.

The student should have familiarity with: data acquisition systems, LabView, CAD software (preferably ProE or Solidworks), and ANSYS
It is desirable that the student have experience measuring pressure, flow, and vibration. This type of experience is typically provided in an undergraduate experimental engineering methods class.

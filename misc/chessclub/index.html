Books about Chess

There are lots of books about chess, many written by famous chess players. Believe it or not, however,  "The Complete Idiot's Guide to Chess" is highly recommended. It also gets rave reviews at Amazon.com (5/5 stars). Unfortunately, when I got to the bookstore I accidentally bought "Chess for Dummies" instead (now how could I have made that mistake?). It's OK, but not great (4/5 stars at Amazon).

If anyone has other recommendations (for beginners and especially for children), I'd be happy to list them here.

Chess Resources on the Web

Chess is a game that breeds fanaticism. My search for the word "chess" on the web returned over two million matches (and now there's one more, I guess). There are whole websites devoted to single openings or what Garry Kasparov eats for breakfast. I don't want to reinvent the wheel, but a good starting point is http://chess.about.com/games/chess/ .  It has reasonably well organized pointers to all sorts of chess material. The "How to Play" link has a nice overview of the rules, both for kids and for adults trying to keep up with kids.

If anyone else knows of a good one, I'll be happy to add it.

Computer Chess Programs

It's not hard to find a computer chess game that can beat you every time. There are plenty of free chess programs that can consistently beat anyone below the Master level. This is because they look many moves in the future and never make a mistake. In most cases the only way to make them play "weaker" is to limit the number of moves that they consider and/or the amount of time they take to consider them, but this is still very different than playing a human being and of questionable teaching value.

The challenge is finding a computer program that plays like a human being and also has decent teaching utilities. Based on admittedly limited experience, I really like "Chessmaster 8000".  Its key features are:

  • Tutorials to learn the basics, many geared specifically toward children.
  • A variety of "drills" at all levels;  that is, it sets up particular situations and you have to find the best move.
  • It can play as any one of a large number of "personalities", each with a particular style ("aggressive", "defensive", etc.) and skill levels ranging from random play to Grand Master Level.  You can even create custom personalities.  When I set it to a personality at my level, I find it indistinguishable from playing a person.
It's very easy to use.  My second grader has no trouble at all with it, and I suspect my kindergartener will be using it soon. You can find the details at www.chessmaster.com. $29.99 at Amazon, but if you look around the web, you can sometimes find it cheaper without the box.  Runs under Windows  (older versions for Mac still available).

There's a great Chess program for kids called Chess Mates.  It starts with the basics and works its way up to some pretty challenging drills.  My kids love it.  I found it bundled with some other programs at Amazon for $17.95. Runs on Windows or Mac.
 

The most popular free chess programs are "GNU Chess" and "Crafty", both of which work with the "Winboard" interface on the PC. This interface was written by Tim Mann, and you can download it and the programs from his web page at http://www.tim-mann.org.

There's also a lot of software info at the about.com site at http://chess.about.com/games/chess/cs/software/index.htm, but some of the links seem to be outdated.

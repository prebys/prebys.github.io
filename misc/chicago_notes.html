Eric's Random Chicago Notes

(Work in progress. Last updated May 16, 2017 by Eric Prebys)

General

Chicago is a fantastic city.  Enjoy it while you're here.  This is by no means a comprehensive list, but maybe some things to get you started.

Getting Around

Driving into town is a mess, and should be avoided.  The train is the best option.  Depending on where you are, you'll either take the Union Pacific West line from Geneva or the Burlington Northern Santa Fe line from Aurora or Rt. 59.   There's an $8 weekend pass that's cheaper than a single round trip ticket.  Trains are pretty sparse on Sunday, and can get very crowded if something is going on in town, so get to the train early.

Once you're in town, you can walk a lot of place, or take the "L" or buses.  If you select the "public transportation" option on Google Maps, it will tell you exactly how to get where you're going.  In town, there are also cabs, and Uber and Lyft work extremely well. 

If you do drive into Chicago, parking is $20-$40, depending on the location.  Definitely use the Spot Hero app to find cheap parking, which can save up to 50% It works directly with almost all the garages in the city, even those in private apartment buildings.

Local secret:  There are two water taxi options that are a very cool way to sightsee and get around at the same time.  The first takes you several locations in the city.  The second services the shoreline and can take you to Navy Pier and the museum campus (Field Museum, Shedd Aquarium and Adler Planetarium).  Both pick up near the train stations. Doing a loop to Navy Pier and the museums is a great option, particularly on a nice day, although the line can get kind of long.

Safety

You hear a lot on the news about how dangerous Chicago is, so it might surprise you to learn that in 2016 (which was a very bad year), Chicago still did not make the top 5 in terms of murder rate (worry a lot more about New Orleans!). Nevertheless, you should be careful.  If  you stick to the "Loop" tourist areas, you'll be as safe as in any big city (see murder map).   Obviously keep an eye on your wallet, phone, camera, etc. particularly in large crowds.   Try to avoid standing alone on an L platform. 

Don't just wander around without talking to a local, because things can change really quickly.  In particular, if you go down to U of Chicago, do not wander out of the college town area!

Things to See and Do

Museums, etc.

The Art Institute is an incredible art museum.  It's famous for impressionists, but also has lots of other stuff, including a cool weapon and armor exhibit.  If you're into art at all, it's easy to spend an entire day there. An easy walk from the train station.

The other three are in a cluster. It's a bit of a walk to get there, but pleasant on a nice day.  Consider public transportation otherwise:
  • Field Museum Natural history museum.  Not to be missed.
  • Shedd Aquarium
  • Adler Planetarium
The Museum of Science and Industry is well South of town, by University of Chicago.  You can get there by "L".  The best thing there is the U-505, a German submarine captured during WWII.  You used to have to wait hours to see it, but now it has timed tickets; however, they run out early on busy days, so either get there early, or see if there's a way to reserve tickets online, because you definitely don't want to miss it.  I personally like their old school exhibits like "fetuses in jars" and "sliced up guy", but these are not as prominently displayed as they used to be (I wonder why), so be sure to seek them out. You can hang out in the U of Chicago area afterward, but be careful not to wander out of the area, because things get really bad really fast.

Places to hang out

On a nice day, just walking around Grant Park and the shore is very nice of nice. Maggie Daily Park is a recent addition next to Grant park.  It's mostly for kids, but there's an amazing wall-climbing complex, if you're into that sort of thing.

Navy Pier is touristy, but fun.  Lots of restaurants and bars.  You can also catch sightseeing cruises from there.  They've recently remodeled it, so it's very nice.  Lots of options for boat cruises from the pier, which are great on a nice day.  There are fireworks off of Navy Pier every Wednesday and Saturday, and also on the 4th (which will be crazy crowded).  There's a Shakespeare Theater on the Pier that's really first rate. 

Magnificent Mile is kind of fun to walk down.  I can't afford to shop there, but it's free to look.  The Apple Store in Manhattan is probably the only Apple Store that's more crowded than the one on the Mag Mile.

Which neighborhood you like depends on your personality.  Here are a few off the top of my head:
  • Rush Street is probably the most famous area.  Lots of restaurants and bars.  A little upscale for my tastes.
  • Lincoln Ave is a bit more hipster than Rush.  More music, less drunks in the streets.
  • Wrigleyville is, as you would guess, near Wrigley Field, and thick with sports bars, but lots of other things too.  A good "something for everyone" option, but don't try to get near it on a game day (unless you're going to the game, of course).
  • Wicker Park/Bucktown.  Weirdly cool.  One of my personal favorites.  When I was a grad student, grad students could afford to live in Wicker Park.  I don't think that's true anymore, but the appearance hasn't changed much. 
  • Old Town.  Another cool area. Home to the Old Town School of Folk Music, which is cool if you're into folk music.
There are lots of others.  You can find more details about Chicago neighborhoods here.

Entertainment


Whatever you're looking for, it's there somewhere. 

You're in Chicago, so you probably need to go to a blues club.  The music is great, but they get crazy crowded, so be prepared.  Here is a list of some of the recommended ones. I used to like Kingston Mines back in the day, but that day was a long time ago.

There's also a great jazz scene.  My personal favorite place is the Green Mill, on the North Side, but there are a zillion others.

There's lots of other music of all kinds, of course.

You absolutely can't leave Chicago without going to the Second City at least once.  This is the place that started it all: Saturday Night Live, SCTV, and pretty much all sketch comedy that followed.  It's much funnier than any of those, mostly because they only have to come up with a new show every couple of months, rather than every week.  There are two stages doing two different shows.  Plan to stay for the improv session after the show.

Finally, Chicago has a vibrant theater scene.  If the big shows are a little rich for you blood, there are lots and lots of tiny theaters, some of which are literally the size of a largish family room.  You can find everything that's playing, as well as deals here.

Events and Festivals in summer 2017

There are some big events in Chicago

  • Ravinia Summer Festival:  This is a very cool and unique Chicago area tradition.  Lots and lots of music all kinds all summer. You can buy seats, or go in for lawn seats.  You can't see the band from the lawn, but you can bring your own food and booze, or buy it there.  Very fun to watch the rich folks try to one-up each other with upscale "picnics" that involve candles and white tableclothes.  
  • Grant Park Music Festival:  Free (mostly) classical music every Wednesday and Friday.
  • Chicago Blues Fest (June 9-11): Awsome, but it's free, and therefore insanely crowded.
  • Wells Street Art Fest (June 10-11):  Fun event.  not sure of the cost.
  • Fireworks at Navy Pier for the 4th of July (July 4): Free, bigger version of the regular fireworks.
  • Taste of Chicago (July 5-9): A Chicago tradition.  "Free", but they tack a surcharge on each strip of food coupons.
  • Pitchfork (July 14-16):  Pretty expensive.  I don't know a single band.
  • Lollapalooza (August 3-6): Lolla always sells out in like 15 minutes.  Tickets available from resellers, but caveat emptor!

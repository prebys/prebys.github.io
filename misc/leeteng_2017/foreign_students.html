Lee Teng Internship Information for Foreign Students

Eligibility

The Lee Teng Internship is open to all students enrolled at US Universities, regardless of nationality, provided they are legally attending the university and are legally allowed to work in the US, with the exception of those born in countries that have been designated "State Sponsors of Terror" (SST) - currently Iran, Syria, and Soudan.  Students born in one of those countries are not eligible for this internship unless they are currently American citizens; that is, those who have become citizens of another country remain inelegible, even it that third country is not on the SST list.

For those students who are eligible, citizenship and/or country of origin play no part in our selection process. 

Work Authorization Requirements

To the students who are admitted to the program, we will offer whatever advice and assistance we can regarding work authorization requirements, with the understanding that the students themselves are ultimately responsible for understanding and satisfying those requirements.  Let me make this clear: we will not initiate any work authorization formalities on your behalf. 

We can make no promises, but as of 2016, we admitted 98 students into the Lee Teng Program over 9 years, of which 38 were foreign nationals, representing 15 different countries.  Of those 38, we only had two serious visa issues. In both cases, it was because the student ignored all our advice and reminders and simply neglected to start the authorization process at all.  In no case has a student properly applied for authorization and failed to obtain it (although we've cut it close a few times).  On the other hand, two problems is two too many, which is why I made this page.

The requirements for authorization to work at one of the labs depend on your particular visa type and the disposition of your university regarding summer internships.  For this reason, if you are admitted to the program, we strongly advise you to contact the foreign affairs office at your university as soon as possible. 

Here are the general requirements for the types of visas we've encountered, in order of how common they are, with the caveat that you should verify information relevant to your own situation for yourself:
  • F1 Student Visa (the most common):  There are two possibilities for F1 students: an easy one and a hard one.  Which one you must pursue depends entirely on your university.
    • CPT (Easy):  We believe that internships like ours are an integral part of a technical or scientific education, and most universities agree. If they do, then the "Designated Signature Officer" (DSO) at the university must sign a "Curricular  Practical Training" (CPT) authorization on your I-20. No further authorization or review is required.  Note, however, that the amount of paperwork required for a CPT varies greatly from university to university, from trivial to fairly significant.  We will do everything we can to convince the university that our internship qualifies as CPT, but the decision is ultimately up to them, which may result in...
    • OPT (Hard): If the university does not agree with this assessment, then you must apply to the US Customs and Immigration Service for an "Optional Practical Training" (OPT) authorization.  This costs money, and takes time. If you find yourself in this situation, you need to submit the paperwork as soon as possible. 
  • J1 Student Visa: 
    • "Academic Training": students on a J1 visa must obtain a signed "Academic Training" authorization.  This is like the CPT, but the standard is less stringent: the internship must simply be related to their education, rather than an integal part of it.  We feel our internship easily satisfies this requirement; however, unlike the CPT, there is no recourse if the university does not agree with this assessment.  There's no good USCIS page for this topic, but here's a description at Lehigh University that's not bad. 
  • Green Card:
    • No further action is required.
  • DACA ("Delayed Action for Childhood Arrivals"):
    • DACA students must have an Employment Authorization Document (EAD)  and Employment Authorization Card (EAC) valid for the period of the internship.  If you do not have already these, you should fill out an I-765 form immediately.

Verification

Like all government contractors, our labs use the "E-Verify" automated I-9 authorization system.  Once you believe you have satisfied the work authorization requirements, you are strongly encouraged to verify your own eligibility at their self-check page.  The law is very clear that we cannot compel you to do this, ask the result, or base any of our decisions on that result; however, by law we also cannot enter your information into our E-verify system until after you start employment.  If at that point, the result comes back that you are ineligible, then not only will you be immediately terminated, but you will have violated the terms of your visa, and once that has happened, it's unlikely we can to anything to remedy the situation.  This in fact happened to a student, and in addition to being terminated, his actions risked expulsion and deportation - which thankfully did not occur.

On the other hand, if we know there are problems with your authorization beforehand, we can work with you and your university to solve them, perhaps even delaying your start date if necessary.  This also has happened with a student, and we were able to avert total disaster in that case, simply by shifting the internship a week later while the issues were sorted out.

Therefore, it would almost certainly be to your advantage to voluntarily inform us of any problems you uncover with your E-Verify self-check, and in all cases to be completely honest with us.

Lab Access

Access to the labs for foreign nationals is a separate issue from work authorization; however, this requires no action on your part other than providing us with any requested information and/or documentation in a timely manner.

Caveat: Beware of stale information!

Computing is constantly changing, and the lab is not particularly diligent when it comes to clearing stale information off of its websites.  If you find yourself looking at a page related to computing where the last update is more than two years ago, chances are very good it's wrong!

To the best of my knowledge, the advice I'm giving here is valid on the date above. If you're stumbling onto this page years later than that date,  then do  yourself a favor and move on!

I'll try to point out things that have changed within the last couple of years, for which you're likely to get stale information elsewhere.

Computing Rules

Fermilab networks and computing resources are there to support work related to the lab's mission.  Reasonable personal use is permitted, provided that it doesn't use significant time or resources.  You can find the full computing policy here, but the most important thing is to absolutely avoid the "four ps":
  • profit - no commercial activity of any kind may be conducted on Fermilab computers.
  • pornography - I hope that one's self explanatory.
  • piracy - no attempt to violate any copyright or licensing agreements will be tolerated!
  • politics - As a government funded organization, none of our resources, including computing, can be used in support of partisan political activity.  This includes lab affiliated email addresses and lab sponsored FaceBook groups.
Violation of these rules can result in disciplinary action, up to and including termination. 

Equipment

Mentors and interns should be in touch with each other well before the beginning of the internship to determine what, in any, computer equipment will be required.  It's the mentor's responsibility to see that these needs are met. We have some spare computers that are available for use, but we find that in most cases, students are most comfortable using their own computers, although they may want a larger monitor and external keyboard/mouse. 

In general, the lab supports Unix/Linux, MS Windows, and MacOSX, and you will likely encounter an eclectic mix of those systems while you're here.

Network Access

When you first arrive at Fermilab, you can connect to the "guest" network by answering a few questions, or to "eduroam" if you have an account, but these are outside of our firewall and have limited access.  Once you have an ID number and services account, follow these instructions to register your device for the "fgz" network, which is inside the firewall and will give you full access to lab resources.  If you're working from outside the lab, you will need to follow the Remote Access instructions below.

Once you have "fgz" access, make sure to remove "guest"  and "eduroam" from your connection list, as accidentally connecting to them will significantly limit your access to lab resources!

Note!  The lab is extremely fussy about what it lets onto the network and is constantly scanning all computers for vulerabilities.  For example, remote login and screen sharing servers will result in your computer being blocked unless you take steps to configure them properly. Unless you really need them, it's best to just turn them off while you're here.  If you get an email warning about a blocking event, you need to fix the problem and follow the link to verify that you've addressed it.  Otherwise, your computer will be auto-blocked.  If you have questions or problems with this, visit the Help Desk in Wilson Hall.

Standard Computer Accounts

There are several basic accounts you will need to work at Fermilab.  Some of these will be covered during your onboarding orientation, but they sometimes leave really important things out, so please read this. 

On your first day, you will be given an accounts on Fermiworks  and Fermi Employee Self Service. You will use these to enter some personal information, fill out your W-9, and set up direct deposit, and then you will likely never use them again during your time here. 

The most important account you will get at Fermilab is your "services account", yourusername@services.fnal.gov.  This is an LDAP username and password that will used to authenticate you on a wide variety of lab services everything, including SharePoint, your time card (more about this in a minute), your email, and most importantly, the Service Now page, where you can request any other accounts or computing services you might need.  You can't really do anything until you get this account.  Your services account should be set up automatically, and they should tell you about it at orientiation.  If they don't, then go immediately to the Service Desk and tell them you need one.  Your services account will have a unique username ("yourusername" in the on this page), which will also be used for other accounts.  In addition to your services account, you will automatically be given
  • a yourusername@fnal.gov email address: This will be forwarded to your personal email.  You can request an actual email address, but it will go away when your internship is over, so it's best to keep using your own in most cases. 
  • a yourusername@FNAL.GOV kerberos principle: This will be used to authenticate you on all unix/linux systems at the lab.
  • a FERMI\yourusername Windows domain account. You can use this to log into any Windows computer at the lab, and it is also used to authenticate you on Samba shared disks. This account will be automatically created, but you may need to call or visit the Help Desk to activate it.

Getting Help (Service Desk, Service Now)

If you have computer problems, you can request help by opening a ticket at the Service Now webpage using your services account, but it's usually more effective to call or visit the Service Desk, and the number is 630-840-2345.  It's located on the ground floor of Wilson Hall, in the North cross over, across from the Credit Union.

Exchange Server (Calendar, Mail, Office, etc)

Major changes recently!

The lab now outsources most of its basic IT services to an offsite Exchange server, hosted by Microsoft.  In particualar, this is where Email is hosted, as well as the calendar that the lab uses for both personal scheduling and to schedule things like meeting rooms.

You can access this server directly by going to http://outlook.office365.com/ and log in by entering "yourusername@services.fnal.gov" as the user and your services password as the password.

You can also sync the system to your own Outlook, as well as various MacOS, iOS, and Android mail and calendar clients by following the instructions here.

Time Cards

Employees need to fill out time cards every week.  You do this using the "kronos" timekeeping service, which you can access here. Log in with your services credentials.  There is an HTML interface at the right which will work from any browser, but it's a bit annoying to use.

The Java interface is better, but can be a bit fussy, and the Kronos site makes it extra confusing.  I've only made it work with Safari and Explorer.  First, go here to verify your version of Java, update if necessary, and make sure it's enabled.

To get Java Kronos to work, do the following:
  • Safari:
    • Go to the Kronos log in page, and log in with your services password.
    • You will get an error in red telling you that Java is not installed, and a link to download a very outdated version. Do not do this!
    • Go to "Preferences->Security-Plug-in Settings->Java", and turn fermilab.kronos.net to "On". 
  • Explorer:
    • Go to the Kronos log in page, and log in with your services password.
    • You will get an error in red telling you that Java is not installed, and a link to download a very outdated version. Do not do this!
    • Go to "Tools->Compatibility View Settings".  You should see "kronos.net" in the top line.  Click "Add" and close.
In both cases, you will have to OK a number of splashes before Java actually runs the first time.

I've been told that you can get it to work with Firefox on Linux using the Iced Tea plugin, but I have not personally made this work.

Go to "My information->My timecard" to access  your time card, then enter your arrival and departure time each day.

All interns are authorized to work 8 hours per day, 40 hours per week (July 4th is an upaid holiday).  Any deviation from that requires supervisor approval beforehand.  You will automatically be given a half hour unpaid lunch, so adjust your arrival and/or departure times accordingly.

Important! Once you have entered your time, do "Approvals->approve" to send the timecard to your supervisor.

Your timecard must be filled out by 10AM Monday morning so that your supervisor can approve it.  The work week runs from Monday through Sunday, so if you wait until Monday morning to fill it out, you need to select "previous week" in the pulldown (I still make this mistake all the time)!

Printing

To access lab printers, you must be inside the firewall (verify that you're connected to "fgz" rather than "guest").  Find the name of the printer, which is usually printed somewhere on or near it, then go to this page for instructions about how to add it to your system.

Note:  I've found that if you already have a printer of the same brand as the one you're trying to install, it can screw up the automated printer installation (it appears to run successfully, but then no printer is added).  If this happens, submit a help desk ticket at SericeNow and they will be able to solve the problem for you. 

Shared Disks

Many groups at the lab use Samba shared disks.  These are accessed with your Windows FERMI domain credentials.  The two most commonly accessed Samba servers are filesrv01.fnal.gov, which has experimental data areas and web space, and beamssrv1.fnal.gov, which has lots of stuff for the accelerator division.  These can only be accessed inside the firewall or via a VPN.

Note that in many cases, you'll need to be given permission to access shared disk.  These are usually granted to members of a particular project or experiment.  Go to the service now page -> "Request New/Renew Accounts"->"Affiliation\Experiment Computing Account Request", enter your information and select the appropriate experiment/project from the pulldown.  If it's not there, then you need to find out who adminsters the disk and get him or her to grant you access. 

To access these disks
  • On MS Windows
    • open a browser window and enter the server name in the address bar preceded by "\\", e.g. "\\beamsrv1.fnal.gov".  You can add more to the path if you want. 
    • You will be asked to authenticate with your Windows credentials. 
    • Use "FERMI\yourusername" as the username
    • Once you do this, the server will appear under "Network" in your file browser
  • On MacOSX
    • Open the Finder
    • Do "Go->Connect to Server..." and ender the servername, preceded by "smb://", e.g. "smb://beamsrv1.fnal.gov".  You can add more of the path if you want
    • You will be asked to authenticate with your Windows credentials. 
    • Use "FERMI\yourusername" as the username
    • Once you access the server, the disk you access will show up as a mounted disk, and be accessible from your desktop or via /Volumes/
  • On Linux
    • Use "smbclient" or "smbmount", as described here.
    • Use the options "--workgroup=FERMI --user=yourusername".  It will prompt you for your password.

Sometimes, you will have access to a shared subdirectory, but not one or more of the directories above it.  In those cases, you will have to enter the full path to access the area, rather than access the top directory and navigate down. 

Recent change: AFS is no longer supported at the lab, so if you see instructions to AFS mount anything, once again, ignore them.

Personal Webspace

Major changes recently!

Everyone at the lab has a personal web area, that can be accessed in several ways.  In the examples below, replace "y" with the first letter of your yourusername.

You can access it via a Samba shared disk
\\filesrv01.fnal.gov\publicweb\y\yourusername

If you have an account on the unix cluster, you can access it as
/publicweb/y/yourusername

or as the
~mydisk/public_html
subdirectory of your main account

The space is accessed via the web as:
http://home.fnal.gov/~yourusername/

Your personal webspace is accessible from outside the firewall, so be careful what you put there and be especially sure you're adhering to the computing rules!

Remote Access (VPN)

Many resources at the lab, including shared Samba disks and a number of the web pages, are blocked by the lab's firewall.  To access them from outside, you will need to use a virtual private network (VPN) client. 

The lab is set up to work with Cisco AnyConnect, which uses "split stream"; that is, only fnal domain addresses are routed through the VPN.  Go here for instructions on downloading and installing the software.  The Catch-22 is that before you have the VPN installed, you only access the download link from inside the firewall. 

Once you have the software installed, you can connect using your services account credentials. 

Access to Unix Resources

Most of the "serious computing" at the lab is done on linux/unix servers or clusters.  Ask your mentor or supervisor if you will need access to any of these. If the answer is is no, you can ignore this section.

If you need to use unix resources, you should request an account on the central unix cluster, by going to the service now page -> "Request New/Renew Accounts"->"Requst FNALU Account".  Your supervisor will need to approve the request.

All unix systems at the lab are accessed with your kerberos principle, but you will need to be granted access to particular machines.  Your mentor/supervisor should arrange for this. 

Note that some unix systems are only accessible inside the firewall.  To access them from outside the lab, you will either have to use VPN, or connect first to FNALU.

To use unix services, you will need Kerberos tools and an xwindows server on your computer.  You can then connect or copy files with secure shell (ssh) or secure copy (scp), respectively.

This is a bit different on each operating system.  These instructions assume you have administrator privileges on your computer. Note that in all these examples, the capital letters in "yourusername@FNAL.GOV" are important.

MacOSX

The kerberos tools are built-in, but you should download the correct krb5.conf configuration file from here and copy it to the /etc/ directory on your main hard disk.   If you're doing this from the Finder rather than a terminal window, the /etc/ directory is hidden, so you will need to do "Go->Go to folder..." and enter /etc.  It's probably a good idea to make a backup copy of the version of krb5.conf that's already there before replacing.

If you don't already have XQuartz, download it from here and install it.

You can either install the "Kerberos Extras", and and get a ticket for "yourusername@FNAL.GOV", or just do it from the command line in a terminal window:
> kinit yourusername@FNAL.GOV 
and enter your kerberos password when prompted.

Once you've got a ticket you can connect to a unix node with:

> ssh -XKA node.fnal.gov

Once you're connected, you can test the link by typing

> xterm

I've found that sometimes OSX updates will cause problems with XQuartz and you will be unable to open Xwindows from a remote note.  Relinstalling XQuartz generally fixes the problem.

Linux

Xwindows is built into linux.  The kerberos tools may or may not be there.  Try typing
> kinit  yourusername@FNAL.GOV

If it doesn't work, you'll need to install the kerberos utilities for your system.  On Redhat, Fedora, Scientific Linux, etc, you can do
> sudo yum install krb5-workstation krb5-libs krb5-auth-dialog

On Ubuntu, type
> sudo apt-get install krb5-user

Download the correct krb5.conf configuration file from here and copy it to the /etc/ directory on your main hard disk.  It's a good idea to make a backup copy of the previous version.

Once it's installed, get a ticket with:
> kinit yourusername@FNAL.GOV

Once you've done that, you can connect to a unix node with

> ssh -XKA node.fnal.gov

Once you're connected, you can test the link by typing

> xterm

MS Windows

There are several ways to handle kerberos/xwindows access under windows, none of them very good.  Ask your supervisor if he or she has a preferred solution.  Otherwise, the simplest solution I found is to use MIT Kerberos for Windows, PuTTY, and Xming, as described here.

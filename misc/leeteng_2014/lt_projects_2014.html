Fermilab Projects

1.  Evaluation of Total Loss Monitors for Cryomodule Radiation Measurement

Mentor(s): Elvin Harms, AD
Student: Yezhezi "Michelle" Zhang, Smith College

ASTA's primary components are a normal-conducting photoinjector gun, laser for producing ultraviolet light which shines on a cathode in order to produce electrons, superconducting RF systems for accelerating the beam up to ~250 MeV, and a beam transport line containing instrumentation to measure and monitor the electron beam qualities. Making beam measurements and doing off-line analysis to determine values, bringing superconducting RF systems into operation and characterizing cavity performance, developing software tools to automate these measurements, and beam line modeling are some of the activities envisioned to participate in.

2. Study of Spherical Array Target for Long-Baseline Neutrino Experiment

Mentor(s): Alberto Marchionni, AD
Student: Quynh Nguyen, Univesity of Minnesota

Will use simulation code to model target and other beam devices in the NuMI and or LBNE lines.  May also be able to participate in beam studies and/or magnetic field mapping of the NuMI horn.

3.  Simulation of Parametric X-Ray Radiation

Mentor(s):  Tanaji Sen, APC
Student: Todd Seiss, Princeton University

The goal is to develop bright, monochromatic and tunable sources of X-rays that can be used for imaging purposes. Two mechanisms are under study: channeling radiation and parametric X-ray emission (PXR). The student will work on modeling the expected photon yield of PXR taking into account details of the crystal structure and geometry, electron beam energy spread and divergence, scattering processes in the crystal, detector resolution etc. There will also be the opportunity to participate in the channeling radiation experiments and connect the channeling radiation model with the experimental data. The student should have had at least one undergraduate course in electrodynamics and preferably one in condensed matter physics. Proficiency in Mathematica or another programming language such as one of C, C++, Fortran, Python,… would be helpful.

4. Bunch Compression and Emittance Growth Minimization at the Advanced Superconducting Test Accelerator

Mentor(s):  Charles Thangaraj, APC
Student: William Wortley, University of Rochester

The student should be comfortable handling sophomore level (college) maths and will write extensive code to run and analyze the simulation data. If time permits, the student will make some measurement along with the supervisor from the accelerator and the compare experimental data with simulated values. At the end of this project, the student will gain a good introduction to electron accelerators, accelerator sub-systems and more specifically to the bunch compressor which shortens the pulse length of an electron beam.

5. Dielectric sample testing for ceramic insert in high-pressured cavity

Mentor(s):  Katsuya Yonehara, APC
Student: Huy Phan, McDaniel College

A ceramic loaded RF cavity is attractive since it can be smaller volume than the conventional pillbox cavity. An electron cascade is processed on the surface of loaded ceramic in high RF gradient. As a result, it induces the RF breakdown at lower RF gradient than the dielectric strength of ceramics. We propose to fill a buffer gas  in the cavity to terminate the electron cascade. We will measure the RF signal and the breakdown light.  Student will join the experiment and make some analysis by using analysis code (Mathematica, Root, or custom-made written by the standard computer language).

Argonne Projects

1.  Characterization of Growth Rate and Interfacial Roughness of Multilayer Optical X-ray Coatings

Mentor(s): Lahsen Assoufid
Student: Yumeng "Melody" Cao, Smith College

The optics group is building a high resolution angle calibration system for the new APS long trace profiler. The system is currently being designed in collaboration with Deming Shu. Some of the key components have already been procured. We expect to assemble the system beginning of Calendar Year 2014, develop the control software and perform preliminary tests by April 2014. The role of the candidate will consist of implementing and using the calibration tool to calibrate the APS long trace profiler, analyze the data, determine the performance of the system, and report the results. If necessary, the candidate is also asked to determine possible ways to further improve it. This work requires basic knowledge of optics, good experimental and programming skills, and knowledge of math and physics.

The work requires knowledge of basic optics, advanced algebra and geometry, and programing in FORTRAN, IDL or Matlab.

2.  RF Noise Suppression for the APS Storage Ring

Mentor(s): Tim Berenc
Student: Kevin Cook, Giorgia Institute of Technology

Amplitude and phase noise in the Advanced Photon Source (APS) Storage Ring radio-frequency (RF) system has a direct impact on electron beam quality. A large source of noise in the RF system is caused by 60Hz power line harmonic related noise in the 1MW klystrons which drive the accelerating cavities. Presently, conventional proportional-integral-derivative (PID) control is employed in the amplitude and phase control loops. Applying adaptive noise cancelling techniques to the existing RF system has shown improved performance. This project will entail experimental and theoretical development of an adaptive noise cancelling system for the Storage Ring RF systems.

Electrical engineering student with coursework in signal processing (i.e., fast fourier transforms and power spectral densities) and RF concepts.  Strong motivation and independence in experiment and data analysis. Experience with MATLAB/Simulink.

3.  Progress in the Design and Simulation of a 1 GeV Superconducting Proton Linac for a Nuclear Waste Transmutation ADS

Mentor(s): Brahim Mustapha
Student: Arjun Kar, Carnegie Mellon University

We have recently developed a preliminary linear accelerator (linac) for an accelerator driven system (ADS) for the transmutation of nuclear waste produced by reactors.  The prospective student will contribute to the finalization of the design which should be a good learning experience for him or her.  The work will involve the final design optimization of RF cavities, the linac lattice design and finishing with beam dynamics simulations of the linac for different beam currents.

Skills:
Basic knowledge of accelerator physics. Computing and analytical skills

4. XMAT linac design and simulations

Mentor(s): Jerry A. Nolen
Student: Brandon Rayhaun, University of Chicago

We are designing a linear accelerator (linac) for a new Extreme Material (XMAT) facility at Argonne. The facility is for material irradiation using ion beams produced by the linac. The defects to the irradiated materials will be studied using photons from the Advanced Photon Source (APS) at Argonne. This project focuses on the design and simulations of the linac including a Radio-Frequency Quadrupole (RFQ) and several room temperature cavities. Following the linac lattice design, consisting of the optimization of focusing and accelerating forces, end-to-end beam dynamics simulations will be performed for different ion beams. The beam spot must be very small at the final image and a magnetic de-magnifying stage is to be designed using either superconducting solenoids or quadrupole magnets.

This effort is ongoing and the interested student should be able to contribute to any part of the work, which should be a great learning experience and a good introduction to accelerator physics.

Skills:
Basic knowledge of beam optics. Computing and analytical skills

5. Study of High Tc Superconducting MgB2 Thin-Film Coated Radio Frequency Cavities

Mentor(s): Ali Nassiri
Student: Ashley Ernst, Ohio Northern University

Student will work on different design aspects of a superconducting RF structure which will utilize high-TC MgB2 thin film on niobium or copper which will allow cryogen-free operation using efficient cryocooler at 10-15 K. Student will use EM simulation package to design and analyze structure RF performance. Student will have an opportunity to participate in test and characterization of a prototype MgB¬2 coated structure if time allowed.  Student will write and present a final report.

Student should have finished electromagnetism and or microwave engineering basics. Thermal dynamics of mechanical engineering is a plus. Student should have basic computer skills such as data analysis and statistics presentation using excel spreadsheet, or origin and etc.

6. Implementation and Improvement of Bead-pull Technique on Cavity Cells

Mentor(s): Goeff Waldschmidt
Student: Li-Chia Tai, UCLA

The Lee Teng summer project will consist of the development of an automated, software-controlled data acquisition system for the evaluation and testing of traveling wave rf structures. Labview will primarily be utilized to acquire data and to post-process results and will be further developed to facilitate the ease and accuracy of testing.  Various methods, including the nodal shift and bead pull methods, will be evaluated and implemented in Labview for the analysis and tuning of the rf structures.
